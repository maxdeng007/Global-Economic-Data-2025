# Argentina around 2025 – Salaries, Incomes, GDP and GDP per Person

This is the **Argentina “turn”**, in the same structured style as your other country profiles.

---

## 1. Salary Levels in Argentina (2024–2025)

### 1.1 Typical (median‑type) earnings

INDEC’s **“Evolución de la distribución del ingreso”** for 2025 gives two key medians:

- **Median per‑capita income, Q3 2025 (all people)**
  - **ARS 463,333 per month** \[1\].

- **Median income of the employed population, Q2 2025**
  - **ARS 700,000 per month** \[2\].

Using a 2025 market exchange rate averaging **≈ 1 USD ≈ 1,250 ARS** (ARS ≈ 0.0008 USD) \[14\]:

- **Median per‑capita income (all people):**
  - 463,333 ÷ 1,250 ≈ **US \$370/month** → **≈ US \$4,450/year**.
- **Median income of employed workers (good “typical full‑time worker” proxy):**
  - 700,000 ÷ 1,250 ≈ **US \$560/month** → **≈ US \$6,700/year**.

Assuming ≈ 38‑hour weeks (≈165 hours/month):

\[
\text{Median hourly wage (employed)} \approx 700{,}000 / 165 \approx \textbf{ARS 4,240/hour} \approx \textbf{US \$3.4/hour}.
\]

**Working benchmark for a “typical” full‑time worker (2025):**

> **≈ ARS 700,000/month** ≈ **US \$560/month**, i.e. **≈ US \$6,700/year (pre‑tax, using market FX)**.

### 1.2 Average (mean) earnings

From the same INDEC income reports and press summaries:

- **Average per‑capita income, Q2 2025 (all people)**: **ARS 537,024/month** \[3\]\[6\].
- **Average per‑capita income, Q3 2025 (all people)**: **ARS 634,451/month** \[1\].

In USD at the same market rate:

- **Q2 2025:** 537,024 ÷ 1,250 ≈ **US \$430/month** (≈ **US \$5,160/year**).
- **Q3 2025:** 634,451 ÷ 1,250 ≈ **US \$507/month** (≈ **US \$6,080/year**).

Because these are **per‑capita**, not “per worker” figures, they mix workers, children, elderly and non‑workers. The **median employed worker (ARS 700k)** is therefore **better** as a “typical wage” indicator.

### 1.3 Minimum wage

Multiple labour and payroll sources for 2025 report a rising sequence of monthly minimum wages \[15\]\[16\]\[17\]:

- **January 2025:** ARS 286,711/month
- **March 2025:** ARS 296,832/month
- **April–August 2025:** rising from **ARS 302,600** to **ARS 322,000/month**.

TradingEconomics shows it climbing further to **ARS 334,800/month in December 2025** \[13\].

For a simple 2025 benchmark:

> **National minimum wage mid‑2025:** **≈ ARS 320–330k/month**
> → **≈ US \$255–265/month** → **≈ US \$3,100/year** (market FX).

**Relative to the typical worker:**

- **Minimum vs median employed wage:** ~**320k / 700k ≈ 45–50 %**.
- **Minimum vs per‑capita median income:** ~**320k / 463k ≈ 70 %**.

### 1.4 Gender pay gap

From the **Global Gender Gap Report 2025** (WEF):

- **Gender wage gap (Argentina, 2025):** **6.25 %** \[12\].

This is defined as the difference between men’s and women’s earnings as a share of men’s:

> Women earn **about 94 % of men’s wages** on this metric – comparatively **low gap** by global standards, though other local studies point to larger gaps in some provinces and in informal work.

### 1.5 Sector and regional contrasts (snapshot)

Interbanking’s **Informe de Transferencias de Sueldos – Octubre 2025** (reported by Bloomberg Línea, La Nación and others) reports \[10\]\[22\]\[23\]:

- **Average salary credited via Interbanking, Oct 2025:**
  - **ARS 1,483,740/month**, up **35.9 % YoY**, slightly above concurrent inflation (~31 %) \[10\].

This is a **formal‑sector, bank‑transferred mean**, not a population‑wide median. It is **well above** the INDEC median employed wage (ARS 700k), indicating:

- A **long high‑income tail** in sectors like oil & gas, mining, IT and finance.
- Large gaps between provinces:
  - **Neuquén, Chubut, Río Negro** at the top,
  - Several northern provinces around **ARS 450–850k/month** \[22\].

Broad sector pattern (from Interbanking and press summaries):

- **Top pay:** Mining & quarrying, finance, IT – many averages **> ARS 2–3m/month**.
- **Mid‑range:** Manufacturing, construction, many services – typically **ARS 600k–1.4m/month** (formal).
- **Low‑pay:** Agriculture, domestic work, small retail/hospitality – often **ARS 300k–600k/month**, close to minimum.

## 2. Household Income, Inequality and Poverty

### 2.1 Per‑capita and household disposable income

From INDEC’s **Q3 2025 income distribution** \[1\]:

- **Average per‑capita income (all people):** **ARS 634,451/month**.
- **Median per‑capita income (all people):** **ARS 463,333/month**.

In USD (market FX):

- Average ≈ **US \$507/month** → **US \$6,080/year**.
- Median ≈ **US \$370/month** → **US \$4,450/year**.

Using **household structure**:

- **Number of households 2025:** ≈ **14.5 million** \[20\].
- **Population 2025:** ≈ **45.9 million** \[18\].
  → **Average household size** ≈ **3.1–3.2 persons**.

Thus, a **median‑type household** income level:

\[
\text{Median household} \approx 463{,}333 \times 3.2 \approx \textbf{ARS 1.48m/month}
\]

At market FX:

\[
1.48\text{m} ÷ 1{,}250 \approx \textbf{US \$1,185/month} \approx \textbf{US \$14,200/year}
\]

That is a **reasonable benchmark** for a “middle household” disposable income.

Separate CEIC data for **household income per capita** give a **nominal figure around US \$4,525/year** in 2024 \[26\], which aligns well with INDEC’s 2025 per‑capita incomes once you account for inflation and growth.

### 2.2 Poverty – levels and thresholds

INDEC’s official poverty statistics for **first half 2025** \[4\]\[24\]:

- **Poverty rate:** **31.6 %** of the population (≈ 14 million people).
- **Extreme poverty (indigencia):** **6.9 %**.
- **Households below poverty line (LP):** **24.1 %**.

**Poverty line, November 2025 – Canasta Básica Total (CBT)**:

For a **“family type” of four** (two adults, two children) in Greater Buenos Aires \[21\]:

- **CBT (poverty line):** **ARS 1,257,329/month**.
  → At market FX: **≈ US \$1,005/month** → **US \$12,060/year**.

So:

- A four‑person household **below ≈ ARS 1.26m/month** is **officially poor** in GBA.
- The **median household income** we approximated (≈ ARS 1.48m/month) is only **about 18 % above this line**, which explains why **one‑third of people** still register as poor and many others hover near the threshold.

### 2.3 Inequality – Gini coefficient

Most recent consistent values:

- **World Bank / UN data (2022–23):**
  - **Gini ≈ 42.4** (0–100 scale) → **≈ 0.424** on a 0–1 scale \[5\]\[7\]\[25\].
- **OECD Latin‑American Economic Outlook 2025** for Argentina shows:
  - **2016:** 42.3
  - **2023:** 42.4 \[8\].

Trend:

> **High but stable inequality** – above most OECD/EU countries (~0.27–0.33), but slightly below the **worst Latin‑American** cases.

Given:

- A **median employed wage** (700k ARS) **above** the median per‑capita income (463k ARS), and
- Very high formal‑sector means (1.48m ARS Interbanking),

the practical picture is:

- A **significant upper income tier**, especially in formal, high‑pay sectors and big cities.
- A **large lower‑middle and low‑income population** close to the poverty line, especially in the **informal sector and poorer provinces**.

## 3. Argentina’s GDP and GDP per Person

### 3.1 Size, per‑capita levels, and PPP

Worldometers (IMF‑based) for **2025** \[9\]:

- **Nominal GDP:** ≈ **US \$661 billion**.
- **Population:** ≈ **45.85 million**.
- **Nominal GDP per capita:** **US \$14,359**.

PPP‑adjusted (IMF WEO, 2025) \[11\]:

- **GDP per capita, PPP:** **US \$32,820** per person.

So:

> In output terms, Argentina is an **upper‑middle‑income economy**: **≈ US \$14k/head nominal, ≈ US \$33k/head PPP**.

### 3.2 Real growth

- **Real GDP growth, 2025 (IMF/Worldometers):** **4.5 %** \[9\]\[19\].

Private forecasts (BBVA, IMF) range **4–5.5 %**, but **4.5 %** is a reasonable central estimate.

### 3.3 Inflation and prices

CPI data and press reports \[6\]\[27\]\[28\]:

- **Monthly CPI Oct 2025:** +2.3 % (up from 2.1 % in Sept).
- **YTD inflation (to Oct 2025):** **24.8 %**.
- **12‑month inflation (YoY, Oct 2025):** **31.3 %**.
- **Nov 2025 CPI:** another **2.5 % MoM**, pushing the annual rate to **31.4 %** \[27\]\[28\].

Central bank REM survey expectation:

- **Projected inflation 2025:** **29.6 %** \[6\].

In short:

> Inflation is **very high by advanced‑economy standards** but **well below the triple‑digit levels of 2023–24**; Argentina is in a **disinflation phase**, though price growth remains **around 30 %/year**.

### 3.4 Labour market – unemployment

INDEC / TradingEconomics \[29\]\[30\]:

- **Unemployment, Q1 2025:** **7.9 %** (highest in four years).
- **Q2 2025:** **7.6 %**.
- **Q3 2025:** **6.6 %** – lowest since Q4 2024.

So:

> **Unemployment is moderate (~6–8 %)**, trending down in 2025, though **under‑employment and informality** remain structural issues.

### 4.2 Relationships and interpretation

1. **GDP per head vs typical people’s incomes**

- **GDP/head nominal (~US \$14k)** is **≈ 2–3× higher** than the **median person’s disposable income (~US \$4.5k/year)** and about the same as **median household income per person (~US \$4.4k)**.
- A large share of GDP is absorbed as:
  - **Corporate profits and retained earnings**,
  - **Public consumption and investment (defence, infrastructure, administration)**,
  - **Taxes, interest, and depreciation**,
  leaving **only a fraction** as **cash income in households**.

2. **Wages vs household living standards**

- A **single median worker** on **ARS 700k/month** nets an amount only modestly above the **poverty line for a four‑person family (≈ ARS 1.26m)** when covering **several dependants**.
- **Multiple earners** or **social transfers** are therefore crucial for lifting households into the **lower‑middle and middle‑class brackets**.

3. **Inequality and poverty overlay**

- With **Gini ≈ 0.42** and **31.6 % of people below the poverty line**, Argentina has:
  - A **relatively small, high‑income formal tier**, especially in **mining, energy, finance, IT**, and
  - A **large informal and low‑wage sector** near or below poverty.
- The improvement in poverty from 2024 to 2025 (drop of **6.5 points**) is partly driven by:
  - **Real wage gains** in formal segments (still fragile),
  - One‑off measures like the **digital wallet transfer program**,
  - **Moderating inflation** relative to past spikes.

4. **Inflation vs real incomes**

- Nominal wages (e.g., Interbanking mean +35.9 % YoY) have **slightly outpaced inflation (~31 %)** \[10\]\[21\].
- This implies **small real wage gains** on average, but:
  - Gains are **uneven** (better in formal, high‑pay sectors),
  - Many low‑income households still feel **persistent erosion of purchasing power**, especially in food and rent.

## 5. Practical Benchmarks (Argentina 2025)

### 5.1 Individual worker – gross monthly income (ARS)

Very roughly:

- **< ARS 300,000/month**
  - Typically **low income**, often part‑time, informal, or just above minimum wage; **vulnerable** to poverty, especially with dependants.

- **ARS 300,000–500,000/month**
  - **Lower‑middle**: common for junior clerical roles, retail, hospitality, and some skilled blue‑collar work.

- **ARS 500,000–800,000/month**
  - Around the **core middle** range; includes many full‑time formal employees and sits around the **median employed (~700k)**.

- **ARS 800,000–1,200,000/month**
  - **Upper‑middle**: experienced professionals, managers, workers in better‑paid sectors or provinces; comfortably above the poverty line even with dependants.

- **> ARS 1,200,000/month**
  - **High income** by national standards; often senior managers, specialists, or workers in top‑paying industries (mining, finance, IT).

### 5.2 Households – net monthly income (3–4 people)

Given the **CBT poverty line for a 4‑person family (~1.26m ARS)** and median household income (~1.48m ARS):

- **< ARS 1,000,000/month**
  - Generally **below or near poverty**, especially in cities; struggle to cover food, housing, utilities.

- **ARS 1,000,000–1,600,000/month**
  - **Lower‑middle**: above official poverty line but tight; highly exposed to inflation and shocks.

- **ARS 1,600,000–3,000,000/month**
  - **Broad middle class**; can cover typical expenses and some saving, though big‑city rents can still squeeze budgets.

- **> ARS 3,000,000/month**
  - **Comfortable/affluent**; clearly above typical living standards, especially outside the Buenos Aires metro area.

## 6. Short “Argentina 2025” Snapshot

By **2025**, **Argentina** is a **high‑inflation, upper‑middle‑income economy** with **nominal GDP per head around US \$14,400** and **PPP GDP per head about US \$32,800**. A **typical full‑time worker** earns roughly **ARS 700,000/month (≈ US \$560, ≈ US \$6,700/year)**, while the **national minimum wage** in mid‑2025 is about **ARS 320–330k/month (≈ US \$260)**. Median per‑capita income of **ARS 463k/month** and an estimated **median household income near ARS 1.48m/month (≈ US \$14,200/year)** sit only modestly above the **poverty line for a four‑person family (ARS 1.26m/month)**, which helps explain why **about 31.6 % of Argentines remain in poverty and 6.9 % in extreme poverty**. Income inequality is **high (Gini ≈ 0.42)**, with a **well‑paid formal tier and a large low‑wage informal sector**, while **unemployment (~6.6 %)** is moderate but under‑employment is widespread. Inflation has **slowed** to around **30 %**, and nominal pay in the formal sector has **slightly outpaced price growth**, yielding small real gains. Overall, Argentina combines **respectable per‑capita output and 4.5 % real growth** with **structural inequality and a large share of the population living close to or below the poverty line**, making the typical household’s experience **far poorer than headline GDP figures suggest**.
